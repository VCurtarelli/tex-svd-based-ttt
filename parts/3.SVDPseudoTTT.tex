\section{SVD-based SSF estimation}
\label{sec:svd_pseudo_ttt}

As stated, the core of the inverse problem in the TTT is to iterate $\bvs{i}$ and $\bvR{i}$, searching alternatingly for the optimal slowness field and the ray paths, using the travel-time information. While the standard method relies on the regularization being appended to the cost function $E(\bvs)$ as an additive term (\cref{eq:sec2:bvs_i+1_regularized-form}), other approaches are possible and available for study.

Here, we will present the singular-value decomposition (SVD) technique \cite{golub_calculating_1965,chen_treatment_2006}, its applicability for a pseudo-inverse (p-inverse), and how this enables an alternative approach, where the regularization step is disjoint of the primary minimization.

\subsection{Singular value decomposition and the p-inverse}

Given the matrix $\bvR{i}$, we can decompose it through the SVD as
\begin{equation}
	\bvR{i} = \bvU \bvSi \bvV[T],
\end{equation}
in which $\bvU \in \re^{\sz{M}{M}}$ and $\bvV \in \re^{\sz{N}{N}}$ are the left- and right-singular matrices of $\bvR{i}$, forming left and right orthonormal bases; and $\bvSi \in \re^{\sz{M}{N}}$ is a diagonal matrix, with diagonal entries $\lambda_j$ and $\lambda_1 \geq \lambda_2 \geq \cdots \geq \lambda_R = \lambda_{R+1} = \cdots = \lambda_M = 0$ being the singular values of $\bvR{i}$, in decreasing order; where $R = \rank{\bvR{i}}$. Given the assumption $N > M$, the last $N-R$ columns of $\bvSi$ are 0. Although by definition all $\bvU$, $\bvSi$ and $\bvV$ are dependent on the iterator $i$, this relation will be omitted for clarity.

Given $R = \rank{\bvR{i}}$, then $\rank{\bvR[T]{i} \bvR{i}} = R < N$, and this matrix isn't invertible in the strict sense. We denote the Moore-Penrose p-inverse \cite{barata_moore_2012} of $\bvR{i}$ as $\pinv{\bvR{i}}$, given by
\begin{equation}
	\begin{split}
		\pinv{\bvR{i}}
		& = \pinv*{\bvU \bvSi \bvV[T]} \\
		& = \bvV \pinv{\bvSi} \bvU[T]
	\end{split},
\end{equation}
where, for the diagonal matrix $\bvSi$, its p-inverse $\pinv{\bvSi}\in\re^{\sz{N}{M}}$ is its transpose, with all non-zero diagonal entries being reciprocated; and for any full-rank matrix $\bvA$, $\pinv{\bvA} = \inv{\bvA}$. With this p-inverse, the solution to the (non-regularized) problem in \cref{eq:sec2:def_optimization_and_cost-function} is
\begin{equation}
	\begin{split}
		\bvG{i}
		& = \pinv*{\bvR[T]{i} \bvR{i}} \bvR[T]{i} \\
		& = \bvV \pinv{\bvSi} \bvU[T] \\
		& = \pinv{\bvR}
	\end{split}.
	\label{eq:sec3:Gi_proposed}
\end{equation}

It is easy to prove that $\nabla E(\bvs{i+1}) = \bv0$ when using the p-inverse, showing that the obtained slowness field fulfills the requirement for being a minimum (considering the quadratic formulation). We let $\bvU{2}$ as the last $M-R$ columns of $\bvU$, linked to the left null-space of $\bvR{i}$. With this solution, the residual travel-time error is
\begin{equation}
	E(\bvs{i+1}) = \bvt[T]{\obs} \bvU{2} \bvU[T]{2} \bvt{\obs}.
	\label{eq:sec3:primary-cost_with_svd}
\end{equation}

\subsection{SVD truncation}
\label{subsec:sec3:svd_truncation}

In general, smaller singular values have a reduced impact on the reconstruction of $\bvR{i}$, as well as in the minimization problem. However, their impact on the estimation of $\bvs{i+1}$ will be amplified by the p-inverse, over-representing the corresponding basis vectors, and possibly leading to overfitting \cite{vallet_solving_1989,nittscher_svddip_2023}.

This effect can be attenuated by setting some of the smallest diagonal entries of $\bvSi$ to $0$ \cite{song_singular_1999}. This reduces the effective condition number (largest to smallest-non-zero singular values ratio), improving stability in the estimation problem. This also increases the practical null-space, which will be followingly exploited. In contrast, using this truncation introduces some residual gradient error.

We denote $\bvSi[t]$ as a truncated $\bvSi$, with only its first $\tilde{R}$ diagonal entries being non-zero. Trivially, $\tilde{R} \leq R$ for this to be meaningful. Given this approximation, we define $\bvR[t]{i} = \bvU \bvSi[t] \bvV[T]$, and the inverse map function can be approximated as
\begin{equation}
	\begin{split}
		\bvG{i}
		& = \pinv{\bvR[t]{i}} \\
		& = \bvV \pinv{\bvSi[t]} \bvU[T]
	\end{split}.
	\label{eq:sec3:Gi_truncated}
\end{equation}

For this solution, $\nabla E(\bvs{i+1}) \neq \bv0$, but instead
%(\cref{thm:svd-truncated_non-zero-gradient}, \cref{app:appB:proofs})
\begin{equation}
	\nabla E(\bvs{i+1}) = 2 \pts{ \sum_{j=\tilde{R}+1}^{R} \lambda_j \bvv{j} \bvu[T]{j}} \bvt{\obs},
\end{equation}
where the summation happens over the truncated singular values; and $\bvv{j}$ and $\bvu{j}$ are the $j$-th column of $\bvV$ and $\bvU$, respectively. Therefore, the smaller the zeroed singular values are, the smaller the gradient will be, and the closer the obtained solution would be to the space of optimal solutions ($\nabla E = \bv0$).

\subsection{Null-space exploitation}
%todo invert this; first explain for the truncated, then particularize to the non-truncated, and show that for non-truncated bvmu doesn't affect

We define $\bvV{2}$ as the last $N-R$ columns of $\bvV$, corresponding to the zero diagonal entries of $\bvSi$; that is, the columns of $\bvV{2}$ form an orthonormal basis for the (right) null-space of $\bvR{i}$.

Given a coordinate vector $\bvmu \in \re^{\sz{(N-R)}{1}}$, then $\bvs[b] = \bvV{2} \bvmu$ will lie on the null-space of $\bvR{i}$, and therefore we obtain
\begin{equation}
	\begin{split}
		\bvR{i} \bvs[b] 
		& = \bvU \bvSi \bvV[T] \bvV{2} \bvmu \\
		& = \bv0
	\end{split},
\end{equation}
where orthonormality of $\bvV$ and the null diagonal-entries of $\bvSi$ were used. This means that, for any $\bvs{i+1} = \pinv{\bvR{i}} \bvt{\obs} + \bvV{2} \bvmu$, we have
\begin{subequations}
	\label{eqs:sec3:cost-gradient_null-space}
	\begin{gather}
		E(\pinv{\bvR{i}} \bvt{\obs} + \bvV{2} \bvmu) = E(\pinv{\bvR{i}} \bvt{\obs}), \\
		\nabla E(\pinv{\bvR{i}} \bvt{\obs} + \bvV{2} \bvmu) = \nabla E(\pinv{\bvR{i}} \bvt{\obs}).
	\end{gather}
\end{subequations}

With this, both the cost function and its gradient are unaffected by $\bvmu$, for any $\bvmu$. This generalizes the singular solution obtained in \cref{eq:sec3:Gi_proposed} to a solution-space that spans the forward map's null-space, within which the primary cost function $E(\bvs)$ is invariant.

\subsubsection*{Truncated case}
The result in \cref{eqs:sec3:cost-gradient_null-space} is only valid for the non-truncated SVD. If truncation is considered, with $\bvV{2}$ comprising the last $N-\tilde{R}$ columns of $\bvV$, then some residual error and gradient will be present. In particular, the gradient will be
%(\cref{thm:diff_gradient_null-space-exploiting_truncated}, \cref{app:appB:proofs})
\begin{equation}
	\nabla E( \pinv{\bvR[t]{i}} \bvt{\obs} + \bvV{2} \bvmu) = \nabla E(\pinv{\bvR[t]{i}} \bvt{\obs}) + \sum_{j=\tilde{R}+1}^{R} \lambda_j^2 \mu_{j-\tilde{R}} \bvv{j},
	\label{eq:sec3:cost-gradient_null-space_truncated}
\end{equation}
where $\mu_{m}$ is the $m$-th element of $\bvmu$. From this, only the first $R-\tilde{R}$ entries of $\bvmu$ --- the ones relative to the truncated singular values --- interfere in the gradient, these being weighted by the respective singular value squared. That is, the smaller the truncated singular values are, the lesser their impact on the difference in gradient will be.

We will assume that $\bvV{2}$ comprises the last $N-\tilde{R}$ columns of $\bvV$, and the truncated singular values are sufficiently small, so that the summation term (truncation error) in \cref{eq:sec3:cost-gradient_null-space_truncated} is negligible.

\subsection{Secondary minimization}

This information on the (approximate) invariance of $E(\pinv{\bvR[t]{i}}\bvt{\obs} + \bvV{2}\bvmu)$ w.r.t. $\bvmu$ can be leveraged to find an optimal solution within a secondary metric, while preserving the cost function and its gradient through \cref{eqs:sec3:cost-gradient_null-space} (up to the neglected truncation-related error). Given a regularization matrix $\bvD$ as before, we define a new cost function $F(\bvmu)$ to be minimized. We let $\bvmu{i+1}$ be the $(i+1)$-th iteration's optimum $\bvmu$, defined as
\begin{equation}
	\bvmu{i+1} = \argmin_{\bvmu} F(\bvmu) = \norm{\bvD\pts{\pinv{\bvR[t]{i}} \bvt{\obs} + \bvV{2} \bvmu}}^2.
\end{equation}

Since by definition $\bvV{2}$ is a column-orthogonal matrix (meaning $\rank{\bvV{2}} = N-\tilde{R}$), if $\bvD$ is a full-rank matrix then the solution is given by (using the p-inverse notation)
\begin{equation}
	\bvmu{i+1} = -\pinv*{\bvD \bvV{2}} \bvD \pinv{\bvR[t]{i}} \bvt{\obs}.
	\label{eq:sec3:bvmu_solution}
\end{equation}

With this, the inverse mapping matrix considering this secondary null-space exploiting minimization is
\begin{equation}
	\bvG{i} = \pts{\bvI{n} - \bvV{2} \pinv*{\bvD \bvV{2}} \bvD} \pinv{\bvR[t]{i}},
	\label{eq:sec3:minim_solution_proposed}
\end{equation}
where $\bvs{i+1}$ is also obtained via \cref{eq:sec2:generic_solution_s-i+1}. This formulation is also valid without a truncated SVD, by replacing $\bvR[t]{i}$ with $\bvR{i}$, and taking $\bvV{2}$ as its null-space-related columns.

With this solution, the primary cost function $E(\bvs)$ is minimized globally (at most within the truncated SVD), and the secondary cost function $F(\bvmu)$ is minimized within the primary cost function's solution space. Since by definition $\rank{\pinv{\bvR[t]{i}}} = \tilde{R} < N$ (using the assumption that $N > M$), it is guaranteed that there will be a null-space to be explored.

%An important remark is that $\bvV{2} \pinv*{\bvD \bvV{2}} \bvD$ can't be further simplified. By the p-inverse's properties, this would require $\bvV{2}$ to have linearly independent rows, which it can't given it is a tall matrix (either $N > N-R$, or $N > N-\tilde{R}$ for the truncated scenario). Another relevant attribute is that, if $\bvD$ is invertible, the solution for taking the gradient of $F(\bvG{i} \bvt{\obs},\bvmu)$ w.r.t. $\bvmu$, $\bvG{i}\bvt{\obs}$, or $\bvG{i}\bvt{\obs}+\bvV{2}\bvmu$, all result in \cref{eq:sec3:bvmu_solution}.

\subsection{Theoretical comparison}

While the literature method aims to minimize both the primary (travel-time estimation error) and secondary (regularization) metrics simultaneously through a single cost function (as established in \cref{eq:sec2:bvs_i+1_regularized-form}), the proposed technique focuses on minimizing one, and within its solution-space, optimize the second one. Although the obtained formulation from \cref{eq:sec3:minim_solution_proposed} jointly encapsulates both minimizations, they are inherently still sequentially solved in the algebraic developments. Given that the main objective is to correctly estimate the SSF, in the SVD formulation this naturally translates to first optimizing this step, and only once this is achieved, improving the secondary objective.

This comes at a cost of a greater mathematical and computational complexity. Through analysis on the achieved formulations in \cref{eq:sec2:minim_solution_literature,eq:sec3:minim_solution_proposed} for the literature and proposed methods, we can find that the Big O notation \cite{mala_bigo_2022} for both techniques is $\mathcal{O}(n^3)$; that is, asymptotically, both will have the same time complexity growth rate. However, it is known that the SVD of a matrix is a very costly process \cite{li_tutorial_2019}, and it may be less computationally efficient for larger matrices.

To improve this, the information of sparsity of $\bvR{i}$ can be used. Given that each ray will travel only a few cells --- at most $N_{\x} + N_{\z} - 1$ cells will be traversed by any ray in a 2D environment, assuming a well-behaved environment --- then most entries on each row of $\bvR{i}$ will be $0$, and so for $\bvR{i}$ as a whole. This enables using SVD algorithms optimized for this scenario, such as in \cite{sairabanu_parallel_2015}. Another scenario is when $M \ll N$, with a heavily over-modeled environment. In this case, the algorithm in \cite{vasudevan_hierarchical_2019} can be usefully implemented.
