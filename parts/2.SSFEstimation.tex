\section{SSF Estimation and the TTT technique}
\label{sec:ssf_estimation}
As previously explained, the TTT uses the delay --- travel-time, or first-arrival time --- between the wave's generation at a source and its arrival at a receptor to estimate the path traveled, and therefore the wave's speed along the route taken.

Given a ray that follows a path $\bvr[v]$, and an SSF $c(\bvp)$ as a function of space ($\bvp=[x,y,z]$ in Cartesian coordinates); then the travel time along the curve $\bvr[v]$ is
\begin{equation}
	t_{\bvr[v]} = \int_{\bvr[v]} \frac{1}{c(\bvp)} \dd \bvp.
\end{equation}

This is known as the forward map: given an SSF $c(\bvp)$ and an origin (source) and a destination (receiver), estimate the path $\bvr[v]$ taken, and its travel-time (following Fermat's principle). The inverse problem's objective is to obtain the SSF, given a set of travel-times and paths between different sources and receivers within this environment.

\subsection{Environment modeling and discretization}

Under the TTT technique, the approach most commonly found in the literature is to discretize the environment in cells, assuming each cell has a constant speed, and estimating this discrete SSF using the measured travel-times. We assume that the space is divided into a total of $N$ cells along the $\x$ and $\z$ directions, for simplicity. We also assume the presence of $M_{\source}$ sources and $M_{\receiver}$ receivers, resulting in a total of $M = M_{\source} \cdot M_{\receiver}$ rays being produced and traveling through the environment.

Mathematically, this discretization and ray pathing can be written as
\begin{equation}
	t_{m} \approx \sum_{n} r_{m;n} s_n,
	\label{eq:sec2:tm_sum-form}
\end{equation}
where $r_{m;n}$ is the length of the $m$-th ray within the $n$-th cell, $s_n$ is the $n$-th cell's slowness, and $t_m$ is the $m$-th ray's travel-time. In vector form, we rewrite \cref{eq:sec2:tm_sum-form} as
\begin{equation}
	t_m(\bvs) = \bvr[T]{m}(\bvs) \bvs,
\end{equation}
in which $\bvr{m}(\bvs) \in \re^{\sz{N}{1}}$ corresponds to the lengths in each cell of the $m$-th ray (this being usually a sparse vector), $\bvs \in \re^{\sz{N}{1}}$ represents the modeled slowness in each cell, and $\tr{\pts{\cdot}}$ stands for the transpose operator. We explicit the dependency of $\bvr{m}(\bvs)$ on $\bvs$, since the ray-bending effects on the $m$-th ray's path will depend on the slowness field, following Fermat's principle and Snell's law.

We now let $\bvt(\bvs) \in \re^{\sz{M}{1}}$ as a vector of travel-times for the current model, for all $M$ considered rays; and $\bvR(\bvs) \in \re^{\sz{M}{N}}$ is a vertical stacking of all $\bvr[T]{m}(\bvs)$. With this, we achieve
\begin{equation}
	\bvt(\bvs) = \bvR(\bvs) \bvs.
	\label{eq:sec2:bvt_product-form}
\end{equation}

A practical requirement is that $N > M$, ensuring a sufficient resolution on the environment (large $N$) with a small number of measurements (small $M$). We denote $R = \rank{\bvR{i}}$ as the rank of $\bvR{i}$, and it is trivial that $R \leq M$.

\subsection{SSF estimation}
\label{subsec:sec2:ssf_estimation}

Given a vector of observed traveled times $\bvt{\obs} \in \re^{\sz{M}{1}}$, the SSF estimation is done by minimizing the error (defined as the mean squared error) between the modeled (\cref{eq:sec2:bvt_product-form}) and the observed travel times, this being translated to
\begin{equation}
	\bvs\opt = \argmin_{\bvs} E(\bvs) = \norm{\bvR(\bvs) \bvs - \bvt{\obs}}^2,
	\label{eq:sec2:def_optimization_and_cost-function}
\end{equation}
wherein $\norm{\cdot}$ represents the traditional Euclidean ($\ell_2$) norm.

Note that this problem is non-linear: the solution to the ray paths $\bvR(\bvs)$ depends on the slowness field $\bvs$, and in turn the optimal $\bvs$ depends on the achieved ray path solution $\bvR(\bvs)$. Given this, the most common approach is to iteratively solve this problem. That is, given the $i$-th iteration solution $\bvs{i}$ and its respective $\bvR{i} \equiv \bvR(\bvs{i})$ (obtained by solving the Eikonal equation), we have
\begin{equation}
	\bvs{i+1} = \argmin_{\bvs} \norm{\bvR{i} \bvs - \bvt{\obs}}^2.
	\label{eq:sec2:bvs_i+1_basic-form}
\end{equation}

In this scenario, $E(\bvs)$ doesn't have a single global minimum. This can be treated by adding a regularization step, turning \cref{eq:sec2:bvs_i+1_basic-form} into
\begin{equation}
	\bvs{i+1} = \argmin_{\bvs} \bar{E}(\bvs) = \norm{\bvR{i} \bvs - \bvt{\obs}}^2 + \alpha^2 \norm{\bvD \bvs}^2,
	\label{eq:sec2:bvs_i+1_regularized-form}
\end{equation}
in which $\bvD \in \re^{\sz{N}{N}}$ is a regularizing matrix, and $\alpha$ a regularization parameter. The proper choice of $\bvD$ ensures a well-posed problem, improving some quality or metric in the obtained solution $\bvs{i+1}$ at the cost of some error in the modeled travel times. 

The solution to the minimization problem is given by an inverse map $\bvG{i} \in \re^{\sz{N}{M}}$, resulting in
\begin{equation}
	\bvs{i+1} = \bvG{i} \bvt{\obs}.
\end{equation}

In order to obtain the inverse map $\bvG{i}$, the gradient of the regularized cost-function $\bar{E}(\bvs)$ is taken,
\begin{equation}
	\nabla \bar{E}(\bvs) = 2\bvR[T]{i} \pts{\bvR{i} \bvs - \bvt{\obs}} + 2\alpha^2 \bvD[T] \bvD \bvs,
\end{equation}
which, when set to $\bv0$ (which will be a minimum, given its quadratic formulation), leads to
\begin{equation}
	\bvG{i} = \inv*{\bvR[T]{i} \bvR{i} + \alpha^2 \bvD[T] \bvD} \bvR[T]{i}.
	\label{eq:sec2:minim_solution_literature}
\end{equation}

This assumes the inversibility of $\bvR[T]{i} \bvR{i} + \alpha^2 \bvD[T] \bvD$. Given the construction of the problem, $\bvR[T]{i} \bvR{i}$ can't be full-rank (since $\rank{\bvR[T]{i} \bvR{i}} = R \leq M < N$), requiring $\bvD$ to be a full-rank matrix. When this condition isn't met, non-inverting schemes have to be used, such as the popularly employed conjugate gradient method \cite{ali_opensource_2019,hormati_robust_2010,tang_travel_2024}.

As explained, the regularizing matrix $\bvD$ (and its factor $\alpha$) have to be appropriately chosen to improve some desired metric. For example, choosing $\bvD = \bvI{N}$ (the $\sz{N}{N}$ identity matrix) results in the minimization weighting in the mean-squared value of the slowness vector \cite{phillips_traveltime_1991}; this is equivalent to considering the presence of white noise in the measurements, and trading model accurateness for noise rejection \cite{pan_theoretical_2015}. Alternatively, $\bvD$ can be chosen to (discretely) approximate the Laplacian of the underlying continuous slowness field \cite{ali_opensource_2019}, leading to a minimization that penalizes the SSF's curvature, resulting in a smoother field.
