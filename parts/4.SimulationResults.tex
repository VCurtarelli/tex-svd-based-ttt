To compare in practice the two techniques presented, simulated SSFs were created, in which these techniques were employed for the inversion step of estimating the SSF. Namely, we will compare the literature standard technique from \cref{eq:sec2:minim_solution_literature} (named TTT), with the proposed method as obtained in \cref{eq:sec3:minim_solution_proposed} (named SVD). In this section, we present the environment and practical conditions assumed, detail the considerations taken for the exhibited techniques, as well as other underlying necessary parameters, methods, and metrics; and finally, we bring forth the results, and their following discussions.

\subsection{Environment conditions}

We assume that the environment is discretized into 2D cells along the width ($\x$-axis) and depth ($\z$-axis), with $N = N_{\x} \cdot N_{\z}$. This enables an easier visualization and faster computational complexity, without oversimplifying the presented conditions. For the simulations, we take $N_{\x} = \pyNx$, $N_{\z} = \pyNz$, and $N = \pyN$.

We also consider the sound speed field to follow the formula derived by \citeauthor{delgrosso_new_1974} \cite{delgrosso_new_1974}, with empirical functions for the temperature and salinity following the thermocline and halocline \cite{janecki_new_2022}. The considered SSF is presented in \cref{subfig:sec4:discretized_ssf_with_profiles:fig1}, with its profiles --- each 1D slice for a fixed $\x$-axis offset --- in \cref{subfig:sec4:discretized_ssf_with_profiles:fig2}.

Along with the SSF, we also consider two distributions of devices, presented in \cref{subfig:sec4:discretized_ssf_with_profiles:fig3,subfig:sec4:discretized_ssf_with_profiles:fig4}: in the first case, the sources are along the bottom and right-side, with receivers along the surface and left-side (which will be called the ``surround'' scenario); and in the second case, the sources are strictly along the bottom, and receivers only on the surface (called the ``layered'' scenario). In both cases and figures, the sources are represented by blue circles, and receivers by red exes. We assume that all devices are at the centered of their respective cells, for simplicity.

The surround scenario more closely aligns with conditions commonly found in the literature \cite{ali_opensource_2019,holstein_acoustic_2004,hormati_robust_2010,jeong_investigating_2024}, specially considering the biomedical applications for this technique. It will also work as a benchmark for the performance of the two presented methods. Meanwhile, the layered scenario aligns with the practical underlying motivation, where ocean bottom nodes (OBNs) \cite{obns} --- commonly used as receivers for oceanic underground imaging --- are repurposed as sources for oceanic above-ground environment modeling, and remotely operated vehicles traversing in the surface are used as receivers. This oceanic water SSF reconstruction is necessary for a faithful imaging of the underground structures \cite{??}.

\subsection{Regularization matrix}

As discussed, a choice of regularization matrix will control which metric will be adjointed to the primary objective of SSF estimation. We chose a discrete Laplacian approach, improving smoothness in the outcome. We assume that $\bvs$ is constructed by vectorizing a $\bvS \in \re^{\sz{N_{\z}}{N_{\x}}}$ matrix, where each entry is its corresponding cell's slowness. More precisely, the relationship between elements of $\bvs$ and of $\bvS$ is
\begin{equation}
	\el{\bv{S}}[j,k] = \el{\bvs}[N_{\x}j+k],
\end{equation}
with $0 \leq j < N_{\z}$, and $0 \leq k < N_{\x}$. With this, $\bvD$ for this discrete Laplacian regularization can be written in block form, with $N_{\z}$ blocks vertically and horizontally,
\begin{equation}
	\bvD = \begin{bmatrix}
		\bvQ' & \Id{N_{\x}} & \bv0  & \cdots & \bv0\\
		\Id{N_{\x}} & \bvQ & \Id{N_{\x}} & \cdots & \bv0\\
		\bv0 & \Id{N_{\x}} & \bvQ & \cdots & \bv0\\
		\vdots & \vdots & \vdots & \ddots & \vdots \\
		\bv0 & \bv0 & \bv0 & \cdots & \bvQ'
	\end{bmatrix}
\end{equation}
where $\bvQ,\bvQ' \in \re^{\sz{N_{\x}}{N_{\x}}}$ are block matrices, given by
\begin{subequations}
	\begin{gather}
		\bvQ' = \begin{bmatrix}
			-2 & 1 & 0 & \cdots & 0 & 0 \\
			1 & -3 & 1 & \cdots & 0 & 0 \\
			0 & 1 & -3 & \cdots & 0 & 0 \\
			\vdots & \vdots & \vdots & \ddots & \vdots & \vdots \\
			0 & 0 & 0 & \cdots & -3 & 1 \\
			0 & 0 & 0 & \cdots & 1  & -2
		\end{bmatrix}
	\end{gather}
	\begin{gather}
		\bvQ = \bvQ' - \Id{N_{\x}}
	\end{gather}
\end{subequations}

\subsection{Metrics and evaluation}

The first metric reflects the primary optimization objective of minimizing the travel-time error, and is given by the root mean squared error (RMSE) of the cost function from \cref{eq:sec2:def_optimization_and_cost-function},
\begin{equation}
	\rmse_t(\bvs{\est}) = \Sqrt{\frac{1}{M} E(\bvs{\est})},
\end{equation}
with $\bvs{\est}$ being the estimated slowness field. We will also use the RMSE of the sound speed field, between the true $\bvc{\true}$ and the estimated $\bvc{\est}$ SSFs,
\begin{equation}
	\rmse_{\ssf}(\bvs{\est}) = \frac{1}{\Sqrt{N}} \norm{\bvc{\true} - \bvc{\est}},
\end{equation}
where $\bvc{\est} = \nfrac{1}{\bvs{\est}}$ (element-wise reciprocal).

$\rmse_t(\bvs{\est})$ reflects the model estimation of the cost function used, while $\rmse_{\ssf}(\bvs)$ represents the underlying objective of estimating the original SSF, as presented in \cref{subfig:sec4:discretized_ssf_with_profiles:fig1}.

\subsection{Estimation parameters}

Although in the definition of the truncated SVD the presented parameter was $\tilde{R}$, we will use a parameter $\beta$, truncating any singular value $\lambda_n$ such that $\lambda_n < \beta \lambda_0$, respective to the largest singular value. For the surround scenario, the TTT technique will use $\alpha = 2.6077$, and the SVD will use $\beta = 0.2888$. For the layered scenario, the TTT will use $\alpha = 7.0179$, and the SVD will use $\beta = 0.6337$. These were chosen through a hyperparameter search, choosing the ones that minimize the SSF error.

Regarding the iterative minimization (alternating forward and inverse maps), the number of iterations will be limited to $20$ iterations, as we empirically noticed no further improvements above this value.

\subsubsection*{Per metric analysis}

It is clear from \cref{fig:sec4:tt_ssf_rms_errors:subfig1} that, at the end of the minimization process, all results (for both techniques, and both scenarios) had very similar travel-time errors of around $0.12\si{\milli\meter}$. It is also noticeable that, while in the surround scenario the travel-time RMSE has a mostly downward trend (with both techniques having similar performances), the performance for the layered scenario reaches its minimum earlier, and then has a growing error later (although this growth is minimal). This potentially indicates overfitting in the results.
