To compare in practice the two presented techniques, a simulated environment was created, and these techniques were employed for the SSF estimation. Namely, we will compare the proposed method as obtained in \cref{eq:sec3:minim_solution_proposed} (named SVD), with the literature standard technique from \cref{eq:sec2:minim_solution_literature_normalized} (named TTT). In this section, we present the environment and practical conditions assumed, detail the considerations taken for the exhibited techniques, as well as other underlying necessary parameters, methods, and metrics; and finally, we bring forth the results, and their following discussions.

\subsection{Environment conditions}
\label{subsec:sec4:environemnt_conditions}

\input{figures/4.discretized_ssf_with_profiles.tex}

The sound speed field was obtained from the World Ocean Atlas (WOA) \cite{woa}. A total of $N_{\x} = 16$ profiles in a location near $-34.5\dg$S, $-51.1\dg$W (in the south of Brazil) were extracted. This location was selected from the WOA database as to bring some variability in the data profiles, while still having consistency between them. Given the limitations of the WOA database, the environment was discretized vertically into $N_{\z} = 29$ cells (downsampled by 2 from WOA data), totaling $N = 261$ cells. A 2D environment is assumed, for simplicity. The considered SSF is presented in \cref{fig:sec4:discretized_ssf_with_profiles:subfig1}, with its profiles --- each 1D slice for a fixed $\x$-axis offset --- in \cref{fig:sec4:discretized_ssf_with_profiles:subfig2}. By convention, the $\z$-axis always points downwards.

We also consider two devices distributions (both with $9$ sources and $9$ receivers), presented in \cref{fig:sec4:discretized_ssf_with_profiles:subfig3,fig:sec4:discretized_ssf_with_profiles:subfig4}: in the first case, the sources are along the bottom and right-side, with receivers along the surface and left-side (which will be called the ``surround'' scenario); and in the second case, the sources are strictly along the bottom, and receivers only on the surface (called the ``layered'' scenario). In both cases and figures, the sources are represented by blue circles, and receivers by red exes. We assume that all devices are at the centered of their respective cells, for simplicity.

The surround scenario more closely aligns with conditions commonly found in the literature \cite{ali_opensource_2019,holstein_acoustic_2004,hormati_robust_2010,jeong_investigating_2024}, specially considering this technique is most commonly used for biomedical applications. It will also work as a benchmark for the performance of the two presented methods. Meanwhile, the layered scenario aligns with the practical underlying motivation, where ocean bottom nodes \cite{cantillo_ocean_2010} --- commonly used as receivers for oceanic underground imaging --- are repurposed as sources for water-column environment modeling, and remotely operated vehicles traversing in the surface are used as receivers. This oceanic water SSF reconstruction is necessary for a faithful imaging of the underground structures \cite{jensen_computational_2011}.

For the forward problem (estimating $\bvR{i}$ given $\bvs{i}$), the \texttt{EikonalFM} Python package \cite{ganster_eikonalfm_2025} will be used, implementing the Fast Marching Method \cite{tang_travel_2024} for solving the first arrival-times along the grid points. This aligns with the standard approach taken in the literature.

\subsection{Metrics and evaluation}

To reflect the objectives of TTT, two error metrics will be used. The first metric reflects the primary optimization objective of minimizing the travel-time error, and is given by the root mean squared error (RMSE) of the cost function from \cref{eq:sec2:def_optimization_and_cost-function},
\begin{equation}
	\rmse_t(\bvs{\est}) = \Sqrt{\frac{1}{M} E(\bvs{\est})},
\end{equation}
with $\bvs{\est}$ being the estimated slowness field. We will also use the RMSE of the sound speed field, between the true $\bvc{\true}$ and the estimated $\bvc{\est}$ SSFs,
\begin{equation}
	\rmse_{\ssf}(\bvs{\est}) = \frac{1}{\Sqrt{N}} \norm{\bvc{\true} - \bvc{\est}},
\end{equation}
where $\bvc{\est} = \nfrac{1}{\bvs{\est}}$ (element-wise reciprocal).

$\rmse_t(\bvs{\est})$ reflects the explicit objective of travel-time error minimization, while $\rmse_{\ssf}(\bvs)$ represents the underlying (implicit) objective of estimating the original SSF from \cref{fig:sec4:discretized_ssf_with_profiles:subfig1}.

\subsection{Regularization matrix}

As discussed, a choice of regularization matrix will control which metric will be adjointed to the primary objective of travel-time error minimization. We chose a discrete Laplacian approach, improving smoothness in the outcome. We assume that $\bvs$ is constructed by vectorizing a $\bvS \in \re^{\sz{N_{\z}}{N_{\x}}}$ matrix, where each entry is its corresponding cell's slowness. More precisely, the relationship between elements of $\bvs$ and of $\bvS$ is $\el{\bv{S}}[j,k] = \el{\bvs}[N_{\x}j+k]$, with $0 \leq j < N_{\z}$, and $0 \leq k < N_{\x}$. With this, $\bvD$ can be written in block form, with $N_{\z}$ blocks vertically and horizontally,
\begin{equation}
	\bvD = \begin{bmatrix}
		\bvQ' & \Id{N_{\x}} & \bv0  & \cdots & \bv0\\
		\Id{N_{\x}} & \bvQ & \Id{N_{\x}} & \cdots & \bv0\\
		\bv0 & \Id{N_{\x}} & \bvQ & \cdots & \bv0\\
		\vdots & \vdots & \vdots & \ddots & \vdots \\
		\bv0 & \bv0 & \bv0 & \cdots & \bvQ'
	\end{bmatrix},
    \label{eq:sec4:construction_bvD}
\end{equation}
with blocks $\bvQ,\bvQ' \in \re^{\sz{N_{\x}}{N_{\x}}}$,
\begin{subequations}
	\begin{gather}
		\bvQ' = \begin{bmatrix}
			-2 & 1 & 0 & \cdots & 0 & 0 \\
			1 & -3 & 1 & \cdots & 0 & 0 \\
			0 & 1 & -3 & \cdots & 0 & 0 \\
			\vdots & \vdots & \vdots & \ddots & \vdots & \vdots \\
			0 & 0 & 0 & \cdots & -3 & 1 \\
			0 & 0 & 0 & \cdots & 1  & -2
		\end{bmatrix}, \label{eq:sec4:construction_bvQ_bvQ':subeq1}\\
		\bvQ = \bvQ' - \Id{N_{\x}}.
	\end{gather}
\end{subequations}

\subsection{Estimation parameters}

Although in the definition of the truncated SVD the presented parameter was $\tilde{R}$, we will use a parameter $\beta$, truncating any singular value $\lambda_n$ such that $\lambda_n < \beta \lambda_1$ (with $\lambda_1$ being the largest singular value). For the surround scenario, the TTT technique will use $\alpha = 3.5133$, and the SVD will use $\beta = 0.2490$. For the layered scenario, the TTT will use $\alpha = 7.0179$, and the SVD will use $\beta = 0.6735$. These were chosen through a hyperparameter search, choosing the ones that minimize the SSF error.

Regarding the iterative minimization (alternating forward and inverse maps), we will limit it to $10$ iterations, as we empirically noticed no further improvements above this value.

\subsection{Metric results}

The $\rmse_{t}$ and $\rmse_{\ssf}$ metrics over 10 iterations, for both techniques (TTT and SVD) and scenarios (surround and layered), are shown in \cref{fig:sec4:tt_ssf_rms_errors}.

In the surround scenario, both methods exhibit nearly identical performance. The $\rmse_{t}$ difference between the two remains negligible throughout the iterations, and both approaches converge to an $\rmse_{\ssf}$ of approximately $1.2\si{\meter/\second}$.

In contrast, the layered scenario reveals clear differences. Both methods present a constant $\rmse_{t}$ behavior over the iterations, with the SVD being marginally (negligeably) worse overall. The SVD approach also achieved a stable and lower $\rmse_{\ssf}$, while the TTT's capability of estimating the SSF worsens over the iterations. This, allied with the stable $\rmse_{t}$, suggests overfitting. Nevertheless, in the layered scenario both methods perform significantly worse than their surround scenario counterparts, with SSF errors more than one order of magnitude larger (at the final iteration).

Overall, the layered configuration leads to fast convergence in the travel-time errors, but is presents poor SSF reconstruction, indicating overfitting to the observed data --- particularly for TTT. While the SVD method mitigates this effect, its performance remains inadequate.

This behavior can be attributed to the limited spatial diversity of ray paths in the layered scenario. The rays propagate predominantly vertically, with limited angular variation, resulting in weak coupling between measurements. Consequently, the inversion becomes underconstrained, leading to solutions that fit the travel-time data but poorly represent the true SSF. In contrast, the surround configuration provides rays with a wider range of directions, increasing interdependence among measurements, and improving reconstruction fidelity.

Since the layered scenario reflects the intended practical application (see \cref{subsec:sec4:environemnt_conditions}), these results indicate that neither method, in its current form, is suitable for this configuration.

\subsection{Estimated sound speed profiles}

\Cref{fig:sec4:estimated_ssfs} shows the estimated SSPs (one profile for each $\x$-axis offset) for both methods and scenarios, with each marker corresponding to a cell. The ground-truth profiles are included in black for reference (replicated from \cref{fig:sec4:discretized_ssf_with_profiles:subfig2}).

In the surround scenario (\cref{fig:sec4:estimated_ssfs:subfig1}), both methods closely match the true profiles, with the largest discrepancies occurring near the surface ($z=0$) and the ground ($z=1500\si{\meter}$). The surface region is known to exhibit stronger variability due to temperature and salinity gradients \cite{janecki_new_2022}; and both regions are less constrained by the regularization, which also affects the reduced accuracy.

In the layered scenario (\cref{fig:sec4:estimated_ssfs:subfig2}), both methods fail to recover the correct profiles. The SVD solution tends toward an almost constant field, while the TTT result exhibits an inverted trend relative to the ground truth. These outcomes are consistent with the metric results, and confirm that insufficient spatial information leads to poor SSF reconstruction. This also suggests that modified acquisition frameworks or additional constraints are necessary for practical deployment in this scenario.
